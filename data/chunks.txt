Formula 1 is the highest class of international racing for single-seater formula racing cars.
Drivers compete in Grand Prix races held on circuits and public roads around the world.
Teams design and build their own cars within strict technical regulations set by the FIA.
Aerodynamics play a crucial role in maximizing downforce and minimizing drag in F1 cars.
Pit stops are strategic moments where teams change tires and make adjustments in seconds.
Tire compounds affect grip and durability, influencing race strategies significantly.
The DRS system allows drivers to reduce drag and increase overtaking opportunities.
Formula 1 cars use hybrid power units combining internal combustion engines and electric motors.
Qualifying sessions determine the starting grid positions for the race.
The Constructors' Championship is awarded to the team with the most points over a season.
The Drivers' Championship is awarded to the driver who scores the most points.
Safety measures like the Halo device protect drivers from debris and impacts.
Weather conditions can dramatically influence race outcomes and tire strategies.
Street circuits like Monaco present unique challenges compared to permanent tracks.
Telemetry data helps teams analyze performance and optimize car setup in real time.
Slipstreaming allows cars to gain speed by following closely behind another car.
Race engineers communicate with drivers to adjust strategies during the race.
Red flags are used to stop races in case of dangerous conditions or accidents.
F1 cars can accelerate from 0 to 100 km/h in around 2.5 seconds.
Energy recovery systems store and reuse energy generated during braking.
